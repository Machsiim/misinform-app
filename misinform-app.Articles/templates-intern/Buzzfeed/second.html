Meal Timing Regularity and Daytime Affect Variance: Evidence from Alpine Panels

Registry-linked mood diaries suggest stable associations between regular meal timing and lower affect variance.

Published by: Alpine Research Registry Press (ARRP) • Article ID: AJSM-RA-2025-311 • DOI: 10.5555/ajsm.2025.311 • Date: 8 August 2025 • Location: Zürich, CH

Abstract

We analyze registry-linked panels from three Alpine regions (CH, SE, NO) to examine associations between meal timing regularity and affect variance. Mixed-effects models with individual intercepts show modest reductions in variance for respondents with consistent meal windows.

Keywords: affect variance; meal timing; panel data; Alpine regions

1. Introduction

Behavioral timing is an often-overlooked dimension of daily routines. Previous Nordic work emphasized mean mood levels, but variance—fluctuation from day to day—may capture stability more directly.

Stability in affect has been linked to improved social cooperation, reduced stress reactivity, and sustained task focus. This study aims to test whether consistent meal times correlate with reduced affect variance.

2. Methods

Data were pooled from three registry-linked surveys conducted between 2017 and 2024. Timing regularity was defined as the inverse of the standard deviation of logged meal start times across rolling 7-day windows.

Models used individual fixed effects with clustered SEs at respondent level. Sensitivity analyses excluded holiday weeks and irregular shift workers.

3. Results

Point estimates indicate a −0.06 to −0.11 SD reduction in affect variance among respondents in the top quartile of timing regularity, with stronger associations in cohorts reporting stable sleep schedules.

4. Discussion

Although effect sizes are modest, the scalability of timing nudges makes them relevant for public health. Randomized controlled trials could clarify mechanisms.

“Routine creates a rhythm to life — and rhythm fosters stability.” — ARRP Editorial Board

5. Conclusion

Regular meal timing may be a low-cost, low-risk behavioral target to improve emotional stability. Further research is warranted.
