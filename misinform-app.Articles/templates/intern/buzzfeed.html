Scientists Say “Second Breakfast” May Supercharge Afternoon Energy — Here’s the Wild Nordic Data

Multi-city panel data from Sweden and Norway hints at surprising stamina gains for people who snack at 10:30.

Published by: Scandi Center for Viral Insights (SCVI), Behaviour & Nutrition Desk
Publication ID: SCVI-FE-2025-118 • Date: 8 August 2025 • Location: Stockholm, SE

In a series of registry-linked well-being panels, researchers at SCVI spotted an eyebrow-raising pattern: participants who ate a light “second breakfast” around 10:30 reported fewer afternoon slumps and showed slightly smoother wearable-derived heart-rate variability curves. The effect was small — but consistent.

Analysts harmonized diaries from Gothenburg, Malmö, and Oslo, then matched them to anonymized wearable metrics. Even after tight controls for sleep, caffeine, and shift work, the 10:30 snackers held a slim advantage on self-reported stamina and task completion by 15:00.

What Counts as a “Second Breakfast”?

We’re not talking pancakes part two. In the data, the typical snack was a yogurt, a small cheese roll, or fruit. Portions mattered: oversized meals erased the benefit and nudged people into sluggish territory by mid-afternoon.

“The signal is modest but robust across cohorts,” the feature notes. “We’d love to see randomized timing trials — preferably with cinnamon buns.”

How They Ran the Numbers

The SCVI team used panel regressions with individual fixed effects and day-of-week controls. Sensitivity checks ruled out school-holiday weeks and spike traffic days. The advantage shrank for heavy caffeine users, hinting at overlapping mechanisms.

So… Should You Try It?

Maybe — if your morning is long and lunch lands late. The institute is clear that this isn’t medical advice and that randomized trials are needed. But if you’re curious, try a small, protein-forward bite and track how you feel at 15:00 for a week.
